Chapter I

Fourteen C (A Crime Scene)

"She did not have assigned seating. She had something better: a system. The system had never failed her. The system had never met Augusto."

Celeste Marchetti had a system.

This is important to understand before anything else happens, because everything that happens next will seem like an overreaction if you don't first understand the system. The system was not a quirk. The system was not a preference. The system was the architecture of a functional adult life, and it was the product of eight months of painstaking refinement, and it governed the 8:12 train to Central Station with the quiet efficiency of a constitutional democracy, which is to say: it had never been formally written down, it was never formally enforced, and yet somehow everybody knew.

Celeste boarded the 8:12 from Waltham East every weekday at 8:11 — never 8:10 (that was too eager) and never 8:12 itself (the door-closing anxiety alone would kill her). She walked to the fourth carriage. She sat in Seat 14C: window seat, left side, optimal sightlines, the overhead light that worked perfectly, close enough to the door to exit efficiently but far enough from the connecting passage to avoid the specific cold draft that came through the seal. She put her bag in the overhead rack. She drank her coffee with her left hand tilted at precisely fifteen degrees to prevent splashing on the acceleration out of Hartley Junction, which the system had clocked at 0.72 seconds of lurch.

Eight months. Two weeks. Four days. The system had never once failed her.

On a Wednesday in February, the system met its first obstacle: a man was sitting in 14C.

He was around thirty. Dark hair. He had one of those tote bags that said something earnest on the side — this one said LITERATURE IS FOR EVERYONE, which is objectively true but feels aggressive at 8:13 in the morning. He was eating a single cracker with the focused attention of someone who considered eating a cracker to be a task worth doing properly. He looked up when Celeste stopped in the aisle. He smiled. Not apologetically. Just pleasantly, the smile of a man who had sat down in a seat and felt no guilt about it whatsoever.

There are other seats. I am an adult. I will sit in another seat. This is not a big deal. I am completely unbothered. I am fine.

Celeste sat in 14B.

14B was categorically inferior. The overhead light flickered every four minutes with the reliability of a broken promise. From 14B you could hear the couple in 13A, who used the commute to process their relationship in real time — today's topic appeared to be whether to get a dog, a subject about which they had very different and loudly held views. The angle to the window was wrong. Everything was wrong. But Celeste opened her book and she was fine.

The man in 14C opened his tote bag.

The tote bag moved.

Celeste did not look. Whatever was in the tote bag was not her concern. People carried things. Things sometimes moved. This was the fundamental nature of things. She did not look.

A sound came out of the tote bag. It was small and soft and entirely unmistakable.

Celeste looked.

Inside the tote bag, nestled in what appeared to be a hand-knitted grey blanket — someone had knitted a blanket. For a pigeon. Someone had done that — was a pigeon. There was a pigeon on the 8:12 train. The pigeon blinked at Celeste with the placid expression of an animal that had never once in its life questioned whether it belonged somewhere.

The Audacity Meter 7 / 10 Elevated. Unprecedented in the 8:12 context. A pigeon.

The man in 14C fed the pigeon a piece of cracker. He noticed Celeste noticing and said, with the calm of someone describing the weather: "Don't worry. She's very quiet. She gets anxious in her carrier, so I bring her in the bag. She knows the route by now."

"There's a pigeon," said Celeste.

"Yes," said the man. He tilted the bag gently so the pigeon could see out the window.

"On the train."

"She likes the view. Particularly between Hartley Junction and the rail bridge — there's a good angle on the river." He said this as if Celeste had simply asked for clarification about the pigeon's scenic preferences, rather than expressed what was by any measure a reasonable degree of alarm.

BRAIN MEMO · RE: THE SITUATION
Current status: A man on the 8:12 has a pigeon in a tote bag. The pigeon has a blanket. The blanket was knitted. Someone sat down and thought: I will knit a blanket. For a pigeon. And then they did that.

"What's her name?" said Celeste, and then immediately hated herself, because asking for the pigeon's name was not the action of a person who was going to successfully not get involved in this.

"Profumo," said the man. He said it the way you'd say the name of an old friend: warmly, with the faint nostalgic note of a shared history.

Celeste stared at him.

"After the scandal," he added. "She has a complicated reputation in the local pigeon community. I thought it suited her."

The pigeon cooed. It was, Celeste had to admit, a deeply dignified sound. She seemed like a pigeon with a complicated reputation.

. . .

"I'm Augusto," said the man, as if this were the natural next thing to say after explaining that his pigeon was named after a 1963 British political sex scandal.

"I know you're not supposed to have animals on this train," said Celeste.

"The conductor knows. His name is Martin. He calls her Profumo too, now. He asked after her last week when she wasn't with me — seemed genuinely concerned. I had to explain she was at the vet. He was relieved." The conductor is relieved. About a pigeon. The conductor has a relationship with this pigeon. This man has brought a pigeon on the 8:12 so many times that the conductor now has feelings about the pigeon's health and wellbeing.

Celeste looked down at her book. The book was a very serious novel about grief and the passage of time and it was extremely good and she had been looking forward to it all morning, and she was going to read it now. She was going to read it and not think about the pigeon or the man or the fact that the conductor was apparently emotionally invested in the continued good health of a bird named after a scandal.

She read the same sentence eleven times.

At Hartley Junction, the train lurched. Celeste's coffee, which was in her right hand instead of her left because 14B had different armrest geometry than 14C and the system had not accounted for this because the system had never needed to account for this, sloshed directly onto her sleeve.

From 14C, Augusto wordlessly produced a small packet of tissues from his jacket pocket and held them out.

She took them.

He didn't say anything. He just went back to looking out the window with Profumo.

I hate it here. He had tissues in his pocket at exactly the right moment. Who has tissues in their pocket? Who is this man?

Celeste cleaned her sleeve. She did not say thank you, because saying thank you would constitute an interaction, and she was not interacting with this man and his pigeon, she was commuting, and commuting was a solitary act.

At Central Station, Augusto stood, carefully repositioned Profumo in the tote bag so her head was facing forward ("she prefers to see where she's going," he explained, to nobody in particular), and stepped off the train. He did not look back.

Celeste sat in the empty carriage for one full minute after the doors closed.

She was going to be late for work.

She was completely fine.

Chapter II

The Problem With Coincidences (There Are Too Many of Them)

"On the third coincidence, it stops being a coincidence. Celeste was on the eighth."

The first coincidence was the train, which Celeste was prepared to write off entirely because the train was a public service and anyone could use a public service and the fact that Augusto was on it was not evidence of anything except the continued existence of the rail network.

The second coincidence was the coffee shop.

Celeste had been going to Rosso's on Gareth Street every morning since it opened, which was going on three years, which was longer than several of her friendships. She had a standing order — flat white, no sugar, in the small cup because the large cup made her feel like she was in a race — and Benedikt behind the counter started making it when he saw her come around the corner. This was her coffee shop. She had seniority.

On Thursday, the morning after the train, she pushed open the door of Rosso's and found Augusto standing at the counter, rearranging the sugar packets.

Not taking them. Not using them. Reorganising them. He had created a clearly alphabetical system — brown raw sugars on the left (Demerara, Raw, Turbinado), whites in the middle, the various branded sticks on the right — and he was adjusting the last few with the careful attention of someone setting a table for a formal dinner.

Benedikt was watching him with his arms crossed.

Celeste stopped in the doorway.

. . .

"I alphabetised them," said Augusto, noticing her. He said it the way you'd explain that you'd held a door open for someone. Friendly, straightforward, completely without the self-awareness that the situation required.

"You alphabetised the sugar," said Celeste. "In a coffee shop that isn't yours."

"The system they had before wasn't a system," said Augusto. "They were just in a pile. A pile isn't a system. It's an absence of a system."

"He has a point," said Benedikt, unhelpfully.

The Audacity Meter 6 / 10 High, but structurally defensible. Benedikt is not helping. "You're the woman from the train. Seat 14B. You moved from 14C after I sat down — I'm sorry about that, by the way. Were you there first? I didn't see anyone." He remembered the seat number. He noticed I moved. He is sorry about this. He is sorry, specifically, about the seat, which is — he doesn't know it was my seat. He doesn't know about the system. He is simply being a person who pays attention to things. I am going to walk out of this coffee shop and find a new one.

She ordered her flat white and stood as far from Augusto as the small dimensions of Rosso's would allow, which was approximately four feet, because Rosso's was the size of a generous wardrobe and four feet was the maximum distance it offered.

Augusto ordered what turned out to be an extremely complicated coffee that involved three temperatures, a specific ratio of milk to espresso that he described using a fraction, and a particular cup. Benedikt made it without complaint. Augusto tipped generously. They appeared to have the rapport of two men who had worked through the complex coffee order together before and emerged the better for it.

He is a regular here. He has been a regular here long enough to have worked out the ratio with Benedikt personally. He is either new to the neighbourhood or has been going to Rosso's for some time without Celeste noticing, which means he's been RIGHT HERE and she's simply never registered him, which is somehow the most unsettling possibility.

This was the second coincidence.

The third coincidence was the library, which happened the following Tuesday and which Celeste would later refer to simply as "the Incident" in a tone that closed all further questioning.

She had gone to return two books and collect one she'd reserved, and she had found Augusto in the reference section, sitting cross-legged on the floor with six books open around him in a ring, taking notes by hand in a notebook. This would have been entirely unremarkable except that he had also, at some point, reorganised the books on the shelf beside him into a different order. Not alphabetical this time. Chronological, she realised, examining them — chronological by subject, within a system of his own devising that he appeared to have written on a small index card, which he had also left on the shelf.

The librarian, passing with a trolley of returns, looked at the reorganised shelf and then at the index card and then said: "Oh, that's much better actually."

The librarian said that is much better actually. The man has reorganised a section of the public library and the librarian's response is that it is much better actually. I am the only person in this situation who appears to find any of this ABNORMAL.

Augusto looked up, saw Celeste, and said: "Third time."

"What?"

"Third time I've seen you this week. I think the neighbourhood is trying to tell us something."

He said this with complete sincerity, the sincerity of a man who took coincidence seriously as a form of information about the world.

"It's a small neighbourhood," said Celeste.

"True," said Augusto. He wrote something in his notebook. "Do you know much about the geography of 18th century land enclosures? I'm trying to work something out."

. . .

She did not know about land enclosures. She collected her reserved book and left. In the doorway she passed a poster for a local history walk, featuring a photograph of Augusto standing on a bridge, pointing at something off-camera with the focused expression of a man who had Opinions about what he was pointing at.

He led historical walks. Of course he did.

She did not attend the walk.

She thought about it for four days.

She did not attend the walk.

The fourth coincidence was the supermarket. The fifth was the post office, where he appeared to know the woman behind the counter's daughter's name and to have genuine and informed views about her recent piano recital. The sixth was the park, where Profumo was perched on his shoulder eating something from his hand, and three other pigeons were clustered around his feet in a loose semicircle that suggested long-established familiarity.

BRAIN MEMO · RE: PIGEON SITUATION UPDATE
There are more pigeons. Profumo is not alone. There is a flock situation developing. Note for follow-up: how many pigeons does one man have? Is there a legal upper limit? Is there a community expectation about an upper limit? Is there a law?

The seventh coincidence was the bookshop, where he was deep in conversation with the owner about a specific printing of a book that Celeste had been trying to find for two years.

The eighth was when she realised she had started looking for him.

She was walking to Rosso's on a Friday morning and she was already scanning ahead for the tote bag — the LITERATURE IS FOR EVERYONE tote bag, or sometimes the one that said THE EARTH IS NOT A RESOURCE TO BE MANAGED, or occasionally a plain canvas one that he used when he had multiple pigeons with him, which was its own category of information she should not have — and when she didn't see him she felt something she could only describe as a specific kind of disappointing absence.

This was the eighth coincidence. Except it wasn't a coincidence anymore.

She was looking for him. She had been looking for him for three weeks. When had she started looking for him?

She walked into a lamp post.

She was fine.

She had a small bruise on her shoulder for a week. She was fine.

Chapter III

His Apartment Was a War Crime (And Also Beautiful)

"He had fourteen pigeons. Each was named after a different historical scandal. She found this insane. She also found herself learning the names."

It happened because of a book.

This is the version of events Celeste has decided on and she is not taking questions. She had been trying to find a first edition of a very specific book for two years, and Augusto had mentioned in the bookshop that he had one, and she had said she didn't believe him, and he had said she was welcome to come and look, and she had said fine, and he had given her the address, and she had spent the subsequent three days telling herself she wasn't going to go. Then she went.

The building was a five-floor walkup on Clemence Street, red brick, the kind of building that had excellent bones and also a lift that had stopped working during a different government. Augusto lived on the fourth floor. Celeste climbed the stairs at a pace designed to suggest she did this routinely and found him at his door with the easy posture of a man who expected the universe to deliver things to him at the time it said it would, which she was realising was simply how he moved through the world.

"You came. I've made tea. Do you want tea? I also have biscuits — the plain kind, not the ones with the jam in the middle, because Watergate ate the last of those on Tuesday and I'm still not over it."

Celeste processed this sentence in order. The tea: fine. The biscuits: fine. Watergate. One of the pigeons was named Watergate. Of course it was.

She crossed the threshold into Augusto's apartment and immediately understood that she was dealing with a profoundly different category of person.

The apartment was, first and most urgently, full of books. Not full in the casual decorative sense of a person who had bought books to display — full in the sense that the books had won. They were in floor-to-ceiling shelves, yes, but also in organised stacks on the floor beside the shelves, and in more stacks on the coffee table, and in a state of careful categorisation that she could see at a glance he had actually thought about: section by century, then by region, then, within that, by something she couldn't immediately identify but which had an internal logic to it. The logic was visible. It was maddening and also sort of beautiful.

The second thing she noticed was the pigeons.

There were so many pigeons.

There were fourteen of them. She counted, because counting was a thing you did when confronted with fourteen pigeons in a domestic space and you needed to anchor yourself to something quantitative. Fourteen. Some were in what appeared to be a purpose-built enclosure along the far wall — roomy, well-constructed, natural light from the window, clearly the work of someone who had spent time designing it. Others were simply out, perched on shelves or on the back of the sofa with the settled confidence of animals who had never been told they couldn't. Profumo was on the windowsill. She blinked at Celeste. She still had a complicated reputation.

On the wall above the enclosure was a small handwritten card for each pigeon, with their name, their date of arrival, and a brief note. Celeste walked over, because she could not not walk over, and read.

WATERGATE — arrived 2022, "found him trying to get into a locked room. classic."

Gerald just looked like a Gerald. Fourteen pigeons, each named with intention and care, and then there was Gerald, who just looked like a Gerald. Celeste stared at Gerald. Gerald stared back. He had an extremely average face for a pigeon.

"He does look like a Gerald," said Celeste, against all her better instincts.

"Doesn't he? I tried to think of a scandal but nothing fit. Some faces are just Geralds."

Augusto brought tea. He sat in the armchair across from her with the completely unself-conscious ease of a person who had people over regularly and whose apartment, with its fourteen pigeons and its floor-to-ceiling books and its internal organisational logic, had never caused anyone to run screaming from the building. Or if it had, he hadn't let it bother him.

The book was real. First edition, 1887, in better condition than it had any right to be. He'd found it in a box at an estate sale and paid three pounds for it because nobody at the estate sale had known what they had, which, he said, was a situation the book itself would have appreciated.

She held it. She turned it over. She looked up and Augusto was watching her with an expression she couldn't immediately categorise — warm, attentive, not intrusive. The expression of someone who liked watching people be interested in things.

He found this for three pounds. He knew what it was immediately. He kept it in good condition. He didn't sell it. He just had it on a shelf with his other books, along with fourteen pigeons named after historical scandals and one named Gerald, and he makes tea for visitors and knows his neighbours and leads historical walks and alphabetises things that need alphabetising and carries tissues because he is the kind of person who notices when someone might need tissues. . . . Oh no.

She stayed for two hours. She did not intend to stay for two hours. They talked about the book, and then about the historical walks, and then about whether it was philosophically possible to have a favourite pigeon (he maintained it was, she maintained it was favouritism, he said favouritism was only wrong when the selection criteria were arbitrary, and his were not arbitrary, they were extensively documented), and then about the neighbourhood, and then about a dozen other things that she later could not precisely reconstruct except that the conversation had the texture of conversations she almost never had, which was the texture of a conversation with someone who was actually there in it, paying attention, thinking.

When she left, Gerald was asleep on top of the bookshelf with one foot tucked under him, and Augusto walked her to the door and said: "I'm glad you came. The book's glad too. It likes being held by people who know what it is."

Celeste walked home.

She was not fine.

For the first time, she was aware that she was not fine, and she was not fine about not being fine, which was a new and entirely unwelcome development.

Chapter IV

The Dinner That Wasn't (Which Absolutely Was)

"She did not agree to dinner. She agreed to a walk that happened to pass a restaurant. They happened to be hungry. Technically nothing was her fault."

She blamed the pigeons.

Specifically: The South Sea Bubble had an ear mite issue, and Augusto needed someone to hold the other pigeons while he administered drops, because pigeons, it turned out, had strong opinions about ear drops and expressed these opinions physically, and he couldn't do it alone. He had texted her — she had given him her number so he could contact her if he found any other editions of note, this was a purely transactional exchange, she stood by this — and she had gone over and spent forty minutes holding various scandals steady while he worked methodically through the treatment, narrating as he went.

"Profumo is the easiest," he said, finishing with Iran-Contra, who had made her feelings about the whole business extremely clear. "She trusts me. Keeler is the worst — she always acts like this is a personal betrayal, which, given the names, has a certain irony." He paused. "Watergate just accepts it. He's weirdly stoic."

"Of course he is. His name is Watergate. He's had time to develop a philosophy."

Augusto laughed. He had a good laugh — short, genuine, the laugh of someone who found things funny rather than someone who performed finding things funny. Celeste noticed this and filed it away in the part of her brain that had apparently started compiling a dossier on him without her authorisation.

BRAIN MEMO · RE: THE DOSSIER
Contents to date: (1) good laugh, (2) carries tissues, (3) tips generously, (4) knitted a blanket for a pigeon [NOTE: confirm whether he knitted it or sourced it — follow-up required], (5) first editions, (6) actual conversations, (7) the way he looks at things, (8) STOP ADDING THINGS TO THIS DOSSIER.

After the ear drops, he made dinner. He didn't announce that he was making dinner. He simply went to the kitchen and began, and said: "You should eat something. You've been holding pigeons for forty minutes and that's physically demanding work and you deserve a meal."

This was not an invitation Celeste could find a logical objection to.

He made pasta. Real pasta — the kind that required a board and a rolling pin and a specific amount of time and evidently a detailed opinion about flour ratios, because she could hear him having one. Profumo supervised from the kitchen counter with the serious expression of a bird with institutional knowledge. Celeste sat at the kitchen table and was handed a glass of wine and a very specific instruction not to move the onions he had set aside because they were doing something important.

The food was extraordinary.

Celeste ate the food and stared across the table at a man who named his pigeons after historical scandals and alphabetised sugar packets in other people's coffee shops and led historical walks and made fresh pasta on a Tuesday for a woman who had come over to hold his birds and somehow, somehow, managed to make all of this seem like the most natural version of a life she had ever seen up close.

. . . "You ordered the wrong thing the last time you went to Marcelline's."

This came entirely out of nowhere.

"I've never been to Marcelline's," said Celeste.

"No, I mean — I saw you there, about a month ago. Through the window. I wasn't following you, I was walking past, I saw you through the window. You had the risotto."

"I love the risotto."

"It's fine. But the pasta al limone is what they do there. It's their thing. The risotto is good but the pasta al limone is the reason the place exists. If you go back, get the pasta al limone." He watched me through a restaurant window for long enough to identify my food. He has an opinion about the correct thing to order at a restaurant I've only been to once. He is telling me this now, over dinner he made from scratch, as if this is a perfectly ordinary thing to mention.

"Have you been to Marcelline's a lot?" she asked, very carefully.

"On their first day. I knew the owner's grandmother — she used to bring preserves to the farmers' market on Clattern Street. I've been going ever since. They know my order."

Of course they do. He knows everyone. He is woven into this neighbourhood at a level of connection I did not know was accessible to an adult human who was not raised here from birth. He has first editions and pigeons and relationships with restaurant owners and a grandmother who brought preserves to a farmers' market and he told me I ordered the wrong thing through a restaurant window with genuine concern about my pasta choices.

After dinner, he walked her home. Not because she asked him to — she definitely did not ask him to — but because he was going that way anyway, he said, which was a claim she was fairly sure was not geographically accurate but which she did not investigate. It was a fifteen-minute walk. They talked the whole time. At her door he said good night in the easy tone of someone who found saying good night uncomplicated, and walked back the way he'd come.

Celeste stood on her doorstep.

The Audacity Meter 10 / 10 Maximum. Historic. He made pasta and walked her home and didn't make it weird. The Meter has never seen a 10.

She went inside. She sat on the sofa. She did not turn the lights on for a while.

She was absolutely, categorically, one hundred percent fine.

Chapter V

I Am Not In Love (This Is a Medical Condition)

"Stage one: denial. Stage two: elaborate denial. Stage three: denial so specific and detailed that it becomes, accidentally, a love letter."

Celeste Marchetti was not in love.

She was experiencing a mild and temporary disruption to her baseline cognitive functioning, brought on by repeated exposure to an unusual stimulus. The stimulus happened to be a person. The person happened to be Augusto. These were facts about the stimulus and did not constitute love. Love was a specific thing with specific criteria and she had not met those criteria. She had a list of the criteria somewhere.

She looked for the list. She could not find the list. She had definitely made a list.

BRAIN MEMO · RE: THE LOVE CRITERIA LIST
Status: Missing. Last seen: three years ago, after the situation with Marcus, which we are not revisiting. Preliminary reconstruction in progress. Criteria may include: shared values, compatible life structures, mutual respect, something about morning routines. NOTE: Augusto has strong morning routine opinions. Noted involuntarily. Filed under: NOT RELEVANT.

She called her friend Priya.

Priya was a person who had known Celeste for twelve years and could therefore be trusted to take the correct view of any situation, which was the view that Celeste was right and whatever was happening was manageable and would resolve itself without requiring anyone to do anything embarrassing.

"I'm experiencing a mild cognitive disruption," said Celeste, when Priya picked up. "Temporary. Stimulus-related."

"You're in love," said Priya.

"I haven't said anything yet."

"You opened with 'cognitive disruption.' That's love. That's specifically your love. Go ahead."

Celeste described the situation. Priya listened. At the part about the pigeon named Profumo, Priya made a sound that might technically have been a laugh. At the part about Watergate being weirdly stoic, Priya put the phone down briefly and could be heard telling her husband about the pigeon called Watergate. At the part about the pasta, Priya said: "Celeste. He made you pasta."

"He would have made pasta anyway. It was a Tuesday. He might make pasta every Tuesday. I don't know his pasta schedule."

"You want to know his pasta schedule."

. . .

She wanted to know his pasta schedule.

I WANT TO KNOW HIS PASTA SCHEDULE. I have become a person who wants to know a man's pasta schedule. Is this who I am now? Is this what I've come to? The system is broken. The system was supposed to protect me from this. The system never accounted for a man who named a pigeon after the Profumo affair and then was completely right about the pasta al limone.

"He sounds wonderful," said Priya, in the tone of someone delivering a diagnosis.

"He reorganises things that don't belong to him," said Celeste. "He alphabetises sugar packets in other people's coffee shops. He has fourteen pigeons. He led me to believe he was going the same way as me and then walked me home and definitely went in the wrong direction afterward."

"He walked you home. The wrong way."

"Yes."

"Because he wanted to walk with you longer."

"Because he has a poor sense of direction, possibly."

"Celeste."

"He has a lot of qualities that are technically problematic."

"Name one that's actually a problem."

He is too comfortable. That's the problem. He is too comfortable with himself and with the world and with me, and I do not know what to do with a person who is entirely without the anxious self-monitoring that I have based my entire adult personality on. He sits in chairs like he belongs in them. He feeds pigeons like it is the obvious correct thing to do. He said "I think the neighbourhood is trying to tell us something" with the calm of someone stating a fact about traffic and I have been thinking about that sentence for three weeks.

She did not say any of that.

"The sugar packets," she said. "Genuinely. In a coffee shop."

"Goodbye, Celeste," said Priya. "Tell him. Or I'm going to text him. I'm serious. Don't think I won't text a man who has a pigeon called Watergate."

"You don't have his number."

"I'll find it. I'll have Benedikt introduce us. He clearly knows Benedikt. Goodbye."

Celeste put the phone down.

She sat in her apartment, which was well-organised and quiet and contained a number of things she cared about and was also, she was noticing, quite conspicuously lacking in any pigeons, any first editions, any handwritten index cards, any evidence of a person who had thought carefully about how to arrange their space as an expression of a genuine interior life.

Her bookshelf was in no particular order.

She had never noticed this before.

She noticed it now and it was, she felt, Augusto's fault.

Chapter VI

Fine. Fine. FINE.

"He told her like he was describing the Tuesday pasta. Completely calm. Slightly proud. Zero drama. She almost fell into the Thames."

The seventh week, on a Saturday, Augusto took Celeste on one of the historical walks.

Not officially. He wasn't leading it. He was just walking her through the part of the neighbourhood that had been a different kind of place in three different centuries, pointing at buildings with the focused delight of someone who had spent a long time learning a language and now found things to say in it everywhere he looked. He knew which house had been a printing press. He knew which corner had been a market. He knew, in several cases, the names of specific people who had stood in specific spots and what they had been thinking, or what they had written about thinking, which was close enough.

Celeste walked beside him and listened and looked at the neighbourhood she had lived in for four years as if she was looking at it for the first time.

"This is the part I always like best. The layers. You're standing on the same ground as sixteen different centuries of people and they all thought this was just where they lived — just ordinary, just theirs — and now you're standing in it and you know all of them were here. It's the closest I've found to time travel." . . .

She stopped walking.

"What?" said Augusto, stopping too.

"Nothing," said Celeste. "Keep going."

He kept going. She walked beside him and she looked at him — not at the street, at him, the side of his face, the way he talked with his hands when something interested him, the way he stopped mid-sentence sometimes because he'd thought of a better word and waited until he had it — and she felt the full weight of the thing she'd been refusing to call what it was for seven weeks pressing down on her chest with the considerable force of something that was not going away.

She was in love with this man.

She was in love with a man who had fourteen pigeons and kept tissues in his pocket and went the wrong way home on purpose and had known what she should order at Marcelline's while watching through a window. She was in love with him and she had been in love with him for approximately six of the seven weeks she'd known him and the first week didn't count because she'd been in denial so profound it had its own structural integrity.

She needed to say something. She needed to say something before this became the kind of thing she'd carry around for the rest of her life and take out in quiet moments to look at and feel bad about. She had done that before. She was not doing that again.

She opened her mouth.

"I've been in love with you since the train."

Celeste closed her mouth.

. . .

They were standing on the corner of Clemence and Whitmore, outside the building that had been a printing press in 1741, and Augusto had just said that, out loud, in the plain and unadorned manner of someone reporting the outcome of a calculation they'd completed weeks ago and had simply been waiting for the right moment to share.

"The train," said Celeste.

"The 8:12. You sat in 14B after I took 14C and I could tell you'd been sitting in 14C — you had a very specific expression, like someone whose coffee had gone cold — and then you asked about Profumo's name and you looked at her with this completely unguarded interest and I thought: I would like to talk to this person for a long time." He noticed. He noticed the expression. He noticed I had been sitting in 14C. He has known since the train — since the first time, since Profumo and the knitted blanket, since the tissues he had in his pocket — he has known since all of this and he has been waiting and he is telling me now in the middle of the street outside a historical building as if this is just the next piece of information in an interesting afternoon.

"You've known since the train," said Celeste.

"Well," said Augusto. "I suspected since the train. I knew by the library."

"The library was the third coincidence."

"I know. I was causing some of them."

"You were causing the coincidences," said Celeste, very slowly.

"Not all of them. The supermarket was genuine. But the library — I knew you went on Tuesdays. I was already planning to reorganise that section." He paused. "The bookshop was also genuine. I really was in the middle of that conversation. But I wasn't not pleased you came in."

He caused some of the coincidences. He engineered circumstances that put him in my path because he wanted to talk to me. He did this by reorganising public spaces and knowing everyone in the neighbourhood and carrying on extended conversations about historical land enclosures with the quiet patience of a man playing a long game. This is the most deeply unhinged form of courtship I have ever encountered. It worked completely.

Celeste looked at him for a long moment. He looked back — calm, steady, without the performance of vulnerability that she'd come to expect from these kinds of conversations, the performed risk-taking that was really just asking someone else to carry the weight of it. He'd said it like it was true and waited.

"I've been in love with you since approximately the second week," said Celeste. "I tried to describe it to Priya as a cognitive disruption."

Augusto smiled. The good smile — not performed, just there. "How's that been working?"

"Terribly. I walked into a lamp post."

"I know. I was there. I was about to come and check on you but you looked like you wanted privacy with the lamp post."

HE WAS THERE. HE SAW THE LAMP POST. HE GAVE ME PRIVACY WITH THE LAMP POST. HE IS THIS MAN.

She laughed. She actually laughed, which she hadn't expected to do, which was somehow the thing that landed the hardest — that even this, the part where she was supposed to be overcome with feeling, came with laughter. He was laughing too. They were standing on a historical corner laughing about a lamp post and the complete disaster of the previous seven weeks.

He put his hand out — not grabbing, just offering, the way he offered things — and she took it, and they stood there for a moment.

"For what it's worth, I think the neighbourhood was definitely trying to tell us something."

Celeste said nothing. She was looking at sixteen layers of history under her feet and thinking about how all of them had stood exactly here and thought it was just where they lived, just ordinary, just theirs.

She thought she understood that now.

Epilogue

The Best Man Was a Pigeon

"It was a small wedding. Intimate. Fourteen pigeons attended. One wore a bow tie. Martin the conductor gave a speech. Benedikt catered the coffee."

Eight months later.

Celeste Marchetti now sat in Seat 14C on the 8:12 every morning, because she lived on Clemence Street now and the 8:12 was her train and she had the window seat and the overhead light that worked perfectly and a man beside her in 14B with Profumo in a knitted blanket on his lap, feeding her pieces of cracker while she surveyed the carriage with the settled authority of a pigeon who had been on this route many times and felt proprietorial about it.

Watergate was the best man. This was not a metaphor. He sat in a specially constructed small harness on the signing table and observed the proceedings with the stoic equanimity that had always been his defining quality, which Celeste now found deeply moving and Augusto had always known she would.

Priya cried, which she said was because of the vows, but which Celeste was fairly sure was also about Watergate. You couldn't look at Watergate in a bow tie and not feel something. That was just the truth of it.

The vows were Augusto's idea. Both sets. He had written his in the same notebook where he took historical notes, in the same hand, with the same quiet seriousness he brought to things he cared about, and when he read them, Celeste thought: this is the kind of person who alphabetises sugar packets because he believes that small things being in their right place matters. He believes it about sugar packets and about library shelves and about first editions that should be held by people who know what they are and about one specific woman on the 8:12 who had a system and had never yet been given a reason to abandon it.

She had a new system now. It accommodated fourteen pigeons, a first edition bookshelf with an organisational logic she had finally decoded (by author birthday, which she'd thought was insane until he explained the reasoning, which was sound), and a man who went the wrong way home on purpose.

"I reorganised your bookshelf," he had said, early on, the first morning she'd woken up on Clemence Street. "While you were asleep. I hope that's all right. It was giving me a headache." It gave him a headache. He reorganised my bookshelf at six in the morning because the disorder was physically painful to him. I am going to spend the rest of my life with a man whose response to a disordered bookshelf is to fix it at dawn. This is fine. This is completely fine. This is the best thing that has ever happened to me.

At the reception, which was in the garden of the building on Clemence Street because the building had an extremely good garden that most tenants did not know about, because Augusto knew the building manager and had been given access years ago in exchange for help restoring the garden's original planting plan, which he had found in an archive, Martin the conductor gave a speech that mentioned Profumo six times and got a standing ovation.

Benedikt had catered the coffee. He had also, because he was Benedikt, alphabetised the sugar.

Gerald sat on Celeste's shoulder for the photographs. He looked, as he always had, exactly like a Gerald.

. . .

Afterward, when everyone had gone and the garden was quiet and the pigeons were settled and the evening light was doing something extraordinary on the city above them, Augusto said: "I think the neighbourhood came through."

Celeste leaned against him. Above them, the city made its indifferent noise — trams and voices and the ordinary sound of millions of lives proceeding in parallel — and below them, sixteen layers of history, all those people who had stood on this ground and thought it was just where they lived.

It was just where they lived.

It was the best possible thing it could be.
